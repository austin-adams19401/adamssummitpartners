Underwriting is where the deal is actually made or lost. Most mistakes in multifamily do not happen at operations. They happen at the model, months earlier, when somebody let an optimistic rent comp or a too-low expense ratio turn a mediocre deal into one that looked acquirable.

Here is how we approach underwriting a Mountain West multifamily property. Not the full model, but the discipline behind it.

## Start with the submarket, not the asset

A Class B asset in the right submarket beats a Class A asset in the wrong one. Before we model a specific deal, we look at the submarket from a few angles:

- **Population growth**: steady is better than spiky. A submarket adding people for ten years in a row is structurally different from one that popped 4% last year.
- **Wage growth vs rent growth**: when rents have outpaced wages for multiple years, the elasticity is thin. We want submarkets where the median working household can still afford the median rent without cost-burden.
- **New supply in the pipeline**: permits pulled, units under construction, units delivering in the next 18 months. Class A deliveries 12 months out are a Class B pricing problem when they hit.
- **Employment base concentration**: a submarket with three anchor employers and a diversified tail is more durable than one tied to a single industry or a single firm.

Mountain West markets vary a lot. Wasatch Front is different from the Treasure Valley. Northern Colorado is different from either. We underwrite each submarket on its own, not as "the Mountain West."

## Rent comps: what to actually look at

The fastest way to lose money is to trust the seller's rent roll without checking. We triangulate every rent assumption against three things:

1. **Our own rent survey of truly comparable properties.** Same vintage window, same unit mix, same neighborhood, same amenities. Not what the broker pulled from CoStar.
2. **Listed asking rents vs actual leased rents.** The gap tells you whether the market is slowing. Asking rents are optimism. Leased rents are reality.
3. **Concessions.** One month free on a 13-month lease is roughly an 8% effective rent haircut. A submarket with widespread concessions is softer than the headline rent numbers say.

If our rent survey comes in below the seller's numbers, we use our numbers. If the seller's numbers are close to our ceiling, we pass on the deal.

## Expenses: the line items where sellers cheat

A pro forma rolled from the seller will often have expense ratios that are structurally too low. The common ways:

- **Property management at 3-4%** when the market rate for a property that size is closer to 5-6%.
- **Maintenance at 8-10%** when a Class B or older vintage runs 12-15% routinely.
- **Capex reserves thin or missing** because the seller capitalized capex differently. We underwrite a real reserve, typically $250-$350 per unit per year depending on vintage.
- **Insurance held at current-year numbers** in a market where insurance renewals have been running 15-30% annually. We underwrite the next renewal, not today's premium.
- **Property taxes held at current assessment** when the transaction itself will reset the assessment. We underwrite a reassessment based on our purchase price, not the seller's basis.

If the total expense ratio coming out of the pro forma is below the market norm for that vintage and class, something is wrong. We rebuild it line by line.

## Exit cap: the hidden assumption that blows up deals

Most of a multifamily return comes from the terminal sale. The exit cap rate is the single most consequential assumption in the model, and it is also the one most often fudged.

Our discipline:

- Underwrite the exit cap at least 50 basis points higher than today's acquisition cap, across the board.
- If the market is frothy and cap rates have compressed recently, widen the spread to 75-100 basis points.
- Stress the exit cap another 50 bps in a downside case. If the downside cap kills the deal, we are not getting paid enough for the risk we are taking.

We never underwrite an exit cap tighter than the cap we are buying at. There is no theoretical justification for that. It is a thumb on the scale.

## The rule that keeps us out of trouble

If the deal only works when we stack optimistic rent growth, aggressive expense assumptions, and a tight exit cap, the deal does not work. Any one of those can move against us. When all three move, the equity is gone.

The rule is simple: **if the numbers only work on aggressive cases, we pass.**

This rule costs us deals. It means we miss some acquisitions that later look fine. That is a feature. The cost of missing a good deal is small. The cost of owning a bad deal is big, slow, and falls on investors we have a direct relationship with.

## Where we actually spend time

For every deal we underwrite, we are putting most of our hours on:

- Rent comp surveys done by humans walking submarkets.
- Expense reconstruction from the operating statements up.
- Exit cap stress across at least three scenarios.
- A walk of the physical property with the property manager, not just a tour with the listing broker.

A model that survives all of that is a model we trust. If it does not, no amount of Excel polish will save it.

This is not revolutionary work. It is just the boring discipline that keeps small operators alive through a full cycle. It is what we owe the investors who put their capital with us.
